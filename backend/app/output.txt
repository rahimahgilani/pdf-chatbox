(Fixes misinterpretation; tone: factual; technique: reframing)

(Reduces rumours; tone: open; technique: engagement + transparency control)

RATIONALE 
1. Why employees are addressed first 
●​ Employees are the most directly affected by the crisis (job security + trust concerns). 
●​ They are currently exposed to the same viral content as the public without internal 
clarification. 
●​ If not informed first, they will rely on social media narratives, increasing internal fear and 
external posting. 
●​ Addressing them first stabilises the organisation before external communication fully 
unfolds. 
Rejected alternative: 
●​ Addressing the public first was avoided because it would increase internal distrust and 
make employees feel deprioritised. 
 
2. Why confirmation bias is central to the strategy 
●​ Employees are interpreting ambiguous phrases (e.g. “cut the dead weight”) through 
pre-existing fears of layoffs. 
●​ Emotional exposure + lack of context strengthens biased interpretation. 
●​ Social media amplification reinforces worst-case assumptions. 
Strategic response: 
●​ Early anchoring of meaning (what the clip is) 
●​ Direct correction of misinformation (no layoffs) 
●​ Reframing as scenario planning 
Rejected alternative: 
●​ Simply “explaining context” was not enough because employees may still interpret 
information through existing fears. 
 
3. Why this communication mode (Email + Slack + 
managers) 
●​ Email provides formal, complete, and permanent clarification. 
●​ Slack ensures immediate visibility during fast-moving rumours. 
●​ Managers reinforce consistency at team level and reduce misinterpretation. 
Rejected alternatives: 
●​ Email only → too slow, allows rumours to spread first 
●​ Slack only → lacks formality and long-term reference point 
 
4. Rationale for strategy steps 
Step 1: Acknowledgment 
●​ Confirms leadership is aware of the issue. 
●​ Prevents information vacuum and rumour escalation. 
 
Step 2: Clarification of clip 
●​ Reframes clip as scenario discussion, not decisions. 
●​ Directly interrupts misinterpretation. 
 
Step 3: No layoffs statement 
●​ Addresses core employee fear directly. 
●​ Removes ambiguity to reduce speculation. 
 
Step 4: Acknowledge language issue 
●​ Shows awareness of emotional impact. 
●​ Maintains credibility without over-apologising. 
Rejected alternative: 
●​ Strong emotional apology was avoided to prevent sounding performative. 
 
Step 5: Employee reassurance 
●​ Restores sense of value and belonging. 
●​ Kept brief to avoid exaggeration or loss of credibility. 
 
Step 6: Open Q&A channel 
●​ Reduces uncertainty and prevents external escalation. 
●​ Provides controlled internal outlet for concerns. 
 
5. Overall tone and framing 
●​ Calm, factual, and non-defensive. 
●​ Limited emotional language to maintain credibility in a high-trust-crisis environment. 
●​ Balanced empathy (acknowledge concern) with clarity (correct misinformation). 
Rejected alternative: 
●​ Highly emotional messaging was avoided because it risks being perceived as scripted or 
insincere. 
 
6. Key persuasion techniques used 
●​ Anchoring: set correct interpretation early 
●​ Reframing: shift meaning from “decision” → “scenario planning” 
●​ Cognitive closure: remove ambiguity about layoffs 
●​ Empathy signalling: acknowledge employee concern 
●​ Authority framing: reinforces message credibility 
 
7. Core strategic rationale (final line for assignment) 
●​ The strategy prioritises early clarification, bias interruption, and controlled empathy to 
stabilise employee interpretation, reduce misinformation spread, and restore 
psychological safety during a high-uncertainty crisis.
